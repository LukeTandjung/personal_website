From Blocks to Buttons: Skeuomorphic Principles from Minecraft Building Applied to UI Design

…A teenage boy obsessed with minecraft, what is new?

I spent most of my teenage years making minecraft builds in creative servers. With enough time and practice, I started making some nice builds.

You can imagine that when I first got into software engineering, and started designing my own user interfaces, I had the horrifically misguided yet not entirely unreasonable impression that I would have an easier time because of basic design foundations I had established in my earlier years.

This could not have been farther from the truth. My first attempt at UI design was quite ghastly.

It’s been a few years since that first UI design. Since then, I’ve taken several more cracks at it, and have made some progress. Heck, the designs for my personal website was the first time I didn’t violently convulse at how ugly it looked. I was even slightly proud of it!

Even so, I couldn't help, but lament.

…Was none of my minecraft building skills transferrable to UI design?

It wasn't until I got nerd-sniped into Linux ricing did I start attempting to answer this question again.

The catalyst for getting sucked into Linux ricing was finding my old 2017 Macbook Air I had used in high school. This toaster had seen better days, and at that point, I was not even sure if it was possible to update it to MacOS Sequoia 15 without it spontaneously bursting into flames. So I made the executive decision to strip MacOS from it and install the NixOS Linux distribution on it.

The term “ricing” was a holdover term from the car enthusiast scene, which stood for “Race Inspired Cosmetic Enhancement”. It was used to refer to cosmetic car modifications that made the car look like it ran faster (like a car spoiler). In the same sense, Linux ricing refers to making your Linux setup look pretty to impress onlookers sufficiently, so that they forgot that you probably installed it on your toaster to get some extra juice out of it.

I thorougly enjoyed ricing my Macbook Air. I enjoyed it so much that I went through the painstaking process of doing it on my Mac Mini.

It was during this particular rice that I had a spark of inspiration:

…Is Linux ricing the missing piece bringing minecraft and UI design?

Let’s start with how minecraft building design works.

A minecraft builder at core aims to build a creative setting that is plausibly realistic. My favourite example of this is a factory built from musical instruments, built by Blockworks Inc.

In such a vast sandbox game unconstrained by material reality, it might seem alittle unimaginative to guide our building process through the lens of realism. But it is exactly this lens of “can this plausibly exist” that allows for secondary belief when looking at a minecraft build, lending to greater immersion in the more fantastical parts of the build.

Definition (Secondary belief): A term coined by Tolkien, it refers to the genuine, immersive belief a reader experiences within a fictional world that allows them to accept its internal reality and fantastical elements. This hinges on having consistent and deep rules set by the author, producing immersion that goes beyond suspense of disbelief.

To this end, Minecraft builders layer realistic visual cues. If you come from a design background, this might sound familiar to you. Minecraft building design sounds similar, in principle, to skeumorphism.

Definition (Skeumorphism): A design style with visual cues (or affordances) that imitate the texture, shape, or function of physical objects.

We can draw parallels between Minecraft builders and UI designers:

  • Minecraft builders layer realistic visual cues in an abstract digital medium (blocks) to generate secondary belief in their setting, then add in their own creative elements.
  • UI designers layer skeumorphic cues in an abstract digital medium (screen plane) to anchor user understanding, then add in their own brand elements.

While helpful, this comparison is still abstract. After all,

…what does layering "cues" look like in practice?

In Minecraft builds, it shows up as a handful of building principles we utilise to organise these visual cues, and make the build look pretty. While the epistemic origins of such principles are unknown, being informally taught and passed down from builder to builder, it does bear an uncanny resemblance to a fusion of Gestalt design principles and art theory. Some of the more relevant Minecraft building principles that have interesting stylistic implications to UI design are as follows:

Depth

A wall in Minecraft with depth almost always looks better than a flat wall. However, there are challenges when it comes to translating this idea from a 3D voxel space to a 2D screen plane. Thankfully, there are multiple way to create an illusion of depth within flat walls in Minecraft.

  1. Shadows: This is something already utilised in skeumorphic or neumorphic design styles.
  2. Colour: Colours of darker values for parts of the wall you want to give the illusion of depth for. This naturally gives rise to a colour hierarchy, Unlike traditional UI design where colour hierarchy is used solely for semantic prioritisation, we now use it to imply physical depth in a 2D screen.

Colour

A block palette is to your Minecraft build what a colour palette is to a UI design. A popular colour palette for UI design is the 60-30-10 colour palette.

However, a Minecraft build usually has a larger palette than the 60-30-10 colour palette. While a build is doable with a 60-30-10 colour palette, Minecraft builds in general look better with more colour variation. A more suitable colour palette that would translate better from Minecraft builds to UI design would be the base16 or even the base24 colour palette. This is a colour palette type that is often used in Linux desktop ricing.

Texture

Texture gives any surface a material quality to it, influencing the feel of the build. From the texture of a single block to creating a specific texture like gradients with multiple blocks, this translates well in UI design! One way we can utilise this is to create grainy background gradients for our UI.

To summarise the Minecraft building design logic, Minecraft building principles are organised to produce realistic cues, which provides a strong foundation for secondary belief in this creative setting. We now have the foundations to attempt to create a design system inspired from Minecraft builds.
