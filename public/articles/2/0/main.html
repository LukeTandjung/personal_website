Your Learning Should Be As Irreverent as Possible

You want to learn something new.

Maybe you want to learn how to draw, or learn a new language. Maybe you want to learn how to cook well.

How do you start? Some obvious answers come to mind.

  • A playlist of YouTube tutorials with marked out timestamps?
  • A structured online course complete with strict learning outcomes, and measured timelines?
  • An instructive and deep textbook with assorted numbered chapters?

All these are convenient starting points. They lower the activation energy required to spur you into action, and stop you from spinning your wheels. For those who are time strapped, this yields the highest return on investment.

Even with all these in mind, I would like to propose something a radical idea to anyone reading this the next time they want to learn something new:

Ditch the framework, ditch the roadmap, the learning outcomes.

Ditch it all.

Meander the fields of knowledge with the wanderlust of a budding journeyman. Ask with the inquisitive curiosity of a baby just brought into conception, conceived into the world. Investigate with the truth-seeking greed of the financial market quant looking for their next big payout.

You should not be afraid of looking stupid. You should not be afraid of offending anyone. You should be as free, and as irreverent as possible.

This line of thinking begets a common response:

If other people have already separate the wheat from the chaff for us, why not just use it?

Therein lies the problem. Because the wheat is not separated from the chaff, for you.

The lesson plan written by the public school teacher is for a class of roughly thirty to forty students. The YouTube tutorial scripted and filmed by the YouTuber is subject to the pressures of audience capture. The short article (the irony is not lost on me!) on a company website is all just part of a marketing pipeline to bring leads in.

In short, nobody really wants to teach you anything.

And that’s fine. It’s better that way, anyway. All this structure is merely an wrapper abstraction over the total sum of knowledge, manufactured for the amorphrous impersonal learner. When you stop imposing that structure on yourself, you force yourself to be explorative, and irreverent. You are no longer constrained by plans and timelines other people set for you. Your learning becomes just another form of recreation, the same way you feel about going to a club or meeting friends for dinner. And like any recreation, you have no obligation to it. You don’t feel pressured to continue when you no longer feel like doing it.

Getting bored from all the reading? Tired and sleepy?

Just stop.

Pick it back up some other time.

Of course, for practicality, you’ll sometimes have to push through boring bits even when you don’t want to, or force yourself through material that would normally interest you on a better day. But on balance, more people probably need to hear this advice than don’t. Most people have had their love of a particular subject destroyed by the rigors of public education, their attention spans fried by short-form content carefully engineered by tech giants to maximize dopamine retention and synaptic burnout. If that sounds like you, you probably need this.

Let your interest guide you. Find something really interesting? Read up on it more deeply.

Find something mind-numbingly boring? Learn just enough to get by and move on.

Free yourself of the obligation to learn from structure that was never made for you.
